\section{Research Objectives}
\label{sec:researchObj}
\vspace{-0.1cm}
The goal of this thesis is to improve upon \gls{UHF} methodology with steady-state acquisitions for two neuroscience applications: \gls{fMRI} and \gls{DMI} (described in \cref{sec:fmri,sec:DMI}). The objective of the first part is to accelerate the proposed passband-\gls{bSSFP} \gls{fMRI}~\cite{bowenHighFieldBalancedSSFP2005,schefflerHighresolutionMappingNeuronal2016} to make it competitive with the established \gls{GE}-\gls{EPI}, which is currently over three times faster. As outlined in \cref{subsec:bssfp_bold}, the primary motivation for \gls{bSSFP}-\gls{fMRI} is its microvascular specificity, similar to other spin-echo sequences, which aims to improve the accuracy of \gls{BOLD} signal localization by mitigating the macrovascular bias and geometric distortion that can compromise the spatial resolution gains achieved at \gls{UHF}~\cite{baez-yanezImpactVesselSize2017,schefflerBOLDSensitivityRapid2019}. Moreover, the short readout times afforded by \gls{bSSFP} reduce $T_2^*$-related blurring and off-resonance-related distortions, both of which are particularly relevant at \gls{UHF}. A 3D stack-of-spirals trajectory was chosen as the preferred encoding scheme to minimize inflow effects while maintaining a balance between acquisition speed and flexibility. Previous functional \gls{bSSFP} studies have proposed highly-segmented 3D EPI~\cite{ehsesMultilineBalancedSSFP2018,millerFMRIUsingBalanced2012} and even spiral readouts without parallel imaging at 3 T~\cite{leeFullbrainCoverageHighresolution2008} to accelerate bSSFP acquisition.  However, current \gls{GE}-\gls{EPI} is a mature and optimized technique employing numerous acceleration strategies, including parallel imaging, partial Fourier~\cite{stengerPartialFourierReconstruction1998}, and sometimes RF encoding via simultaneous multi-slice acquisitions~\cite{moellerMultibandMultisliceGEEPI2010}. Therefore, to achieve state-of-the-art acquisition speed in this work, parallel imaging must be incorporated.  Furthermore, challenges at \gls{UHF}, such as highly off-resonant fat signal, must be suppressed for a low-bandwidth spiral readout, and the residual spatial off-resonance induced blurring must be corrected using deblurring algorithms (described in \cref{sec:deblur}). Given the acquisition of several hundred volumes required for functional studies, the computational cost and memory footprint of the reconstruction model must be minimized. Finally, the isolated effects of \gls{TE}, \gls{TR} and flip angle must be determined to propose high-resolution \gls{fMRI} protocols to maximize the functional sensitivity.

As a second topic, we investigated the sensitivity improvements of \gls{DMI} with \gls{bSSFP} to enable high-resolution molecular imaging at \gls{UHF}. The inherently low MR sensitivity of deuterium nuclei and their low \textit{in vivo} concentrations have historically resulted in glucose-\gls{DMI} investigations in the human brain being performed at low nominal spatio-temporal resolutions (see \cref{subsec:DMIsens}). This clinical translation study was motivated by encouraging preclinical results demonstrating 2--3 times SNR improvement with \gls{bSSFP} compared to spoiled \gls{GE} sequences~\cite{petersImprovingDeuteriumMetabolic2021,montraziHighsensitivityDeuteriumMetabolic2023}, attributable to the favorable $T_2/T_1$ ratio of deuterium metabolites.  
Thus, during this work, two phase-cycled spectral-spatial \gls{bSSFP} methods were developed to address the various challenges that are specific to \gls{DMI} of the human brain at \gls{UHF}. Phase-cycling was employed to reduce the off-resonance sensitivity of \gls{bSSFP} acquisitions. However, phase cycling introduces complex signal modulation that must be accounted for in the signal model during spectral separation. Therefore, accurate signal models are essential for optimizing \gls{SNR} of the measurement protocol and estimating metabolite concentrations. As off-resonance effects due to chemical shifts and spatial $B_0$ inhomogeneity are indistinguishable, estimation of accurate spatial off-resonance maps is warranted for reliable metabolite quantification. The sparse deuterium spectrum, with four observable resonances (water, glucose, glutamate/glutamine, and lactate/lipid) separated by approximately 1 ppm, allows the use of two spectroscopic image encoding schemes: conventional \gls{CSI} and multi-echo. The SNR performance and spectral resolution of these two phase-cycled \gls{bSSFP} variants were compared with the reference vendor’s standard \gls{CSI} implementation in phantom and \textit{in vivo} human studies with oral [6,6'-$^2H_2$]-glucose intake.
