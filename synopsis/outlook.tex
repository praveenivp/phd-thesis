\section{Outlook}
\label{sec:outlook}
Passband-\gls{bSSFP} offers a potential alternative to traditional \gls{GE}-\gls{BOLD} \gls{fMRI} at \gls{UHF}. It leverages the vessel size specificity to isolate \gls{BOLD} signals originating near small vessels such as capillaries to be closer to neuronal activity and to minimize contributions from larger vessels.  In publication 1, we have addressed a key practical limitation, the slow acquisition speed, with efficient spiral acquisition. However, \textit{in vivo} validation of the vessel size specificity is crucial for broader acceptance of \gls{bSSFP}-\gls{fMRI}. While demonstrated in simulations~\cite{baez-yanezImpactVesselSize2017} and phantom experiments with micro-spheres ~\cite{schefflerBOLDSensitivityRapid2019}, replicating these findings \textit{in vivo} remains challenging. A recent resting-state study reported that the time series of \gls{bSSFP} are minimally influenced by surface veins and cortical orientation compared with \gls{GE} sequences~\cite{ramadanMacrovascularContributionsRestingstate2025}. In this context, two preliminary investigations have been conducted in the course of this work to characterize the vessel-size specificity of \gls{bSSFP}-\gls{BOLD} contrast. These are briefly presented in the following \cref{subsec:veins,subsec:funcphasecycling}. The developed 3D spiral-\gls{bSSFP} sequence could enable functional investigations not only at high spatial, but also at high temporal resolution. Notably, the method made it possible to capture a distinctive \gls{bSSFP}-\gls{BOLD} contrast inversion during the brief transient phase, as detailed in \cref{subsec:invBOLD}. On the technological side, the image quality and temporal stability of the time series can be improved with prospective motion correction~\cite{zaitsevProspectiveMotionCorrection2017} and reconstruction methods that take the measured spatio-temporal field fluctuations into account~\cite{graedelFeasibilitySpiralFMRI2021,vannesjoImageReconstructionUsing2016}. In \cref{subsec:concurrentMonitoring}, the ongoing developments as well as future plans of concurrent field monitoring with our newly built integrated field-probe \gls{RF} array is briefly summarized.

The improved sensitivity of the \gls{DMI} methods developed in this work holds promise for advancing cancer diagnosis and treatment monitoring of novel therapies. While the study shown here examines the healthy human brain, future investigations should apply these techniques to characterize tumor metabolism, as already shown for glioblastoma~\cite{defeyterDeuteriumMetabolicImaging2018}. Molecular profiling of small-volume lesions, such as melanoma brain metastases or low-grade gliomas, would further demonstrate the clinical potential of high-resolution \gls{DMI}. To that end, we conducted a pilot study in patients with melanoma brain metastases, and the initial results as well as considerations for future investigations are presented in \cref{subsec:dmipatient}. 

\subsection{Investigating large-vessel bias}
\label{subsec:veins}
In this preliminary study, \gls{BOLD} signal changes acquired with the developed 3D stack-of-spirals sequence were analyzed near large vessels segmented from a co-registered high-resolution Cartesian \gls{GE} scan. The underlying hypothesis is that bSSFP-BOLD should exhibit reduced signal changes near large veins compared to \gls{GE}-\gls{BOLD}, reflecting improved spatial specificity. As shown in \cref{fig:vesselDistance}, \gls{bSSFP}-\gls{BOLD} signal changes near large vessels are marginally lower, suggesting a certain degree of vessel-size specificity. This effect becomes more pronounced in another functional \gls{bSSFP} acquisition conducted with shorter \gls{TR} of 6~ms, where suppression of large-vessel contributions is more clearly visible.  Although these findings are encouraging, the overall reduced \gls{BOLD}-related signal change of\gls{bSSFP} makes it difficult to fully assess the vessel-size sensitivity.

\subsection{BOLD signal change in refocusing modes}
\label{subsec:funcphasecycling}
Demonstrating small-vessel specificity of \gls{bSSFP}-\gls{BOLD} in the human brain is challenging due to the difficulty of imaging the vasculature and its subsequent segmentation. An alternative approach is to examine \gls{BOLD}-related signal changes in the refocusing echo pathways of \gls{bSSFP}, as described in \cref{subsec:bssfp_conf_modes}. In our preliminary investigation~\cite{iyyappanvalsalaInvestigationBSSFPFrequency2024}, high-resolution bSSFP frequency responses were acquired with an RF phase increment resolution of 1.8°, and the corresponding \gls{BOLD}-related signal changes were analyzed in the \gls{SSFP} configuration space. Experimental limitations prevented fully sampled \gls{bSSFP} profiles under both active and resting conditions within two functional runs, due to scanner drifts and the slow hemodynamic response. Nevertheless, a few noteworthy signal features could be still identified. As illustrated in \cref{fig:bssfpBOLDmodes}, the refocusing \gls{SSFP} mode amplitudes ($F_{-1}$, $F_{-2}$, …) in activated voxels with z-scores > 21 exhibited larger signal differences, suggesting that the \gls{bSSFP}-\gls{BOLD} signal changes predominantly arise from refocusing pathways rather than dephasing modes for short \gls{TR}/\gls{TE} (5/1.6~ms). Furthermore, a consistent difference in stopband width between the profiles obtained during resting and active conditions was observed for highly significant activated voxels (z-score > 15). The broader stopband during resting condition may indicate a wider underlying intravoxel frequency spectrum. These findings could be further investigated to better characterize the \gls{bSSFP}-\gls{BOLD} contrast.

\subsection{Transient bSSFP-BOLD}
\label{subsec:invBOLD}
In some sub-optimal passband-\gls{bSSFP} \gls{fMRI} studies, 2D multi-slice \gls{bSSFP} has been proposed to improve coverage ~\cite{reynaudMultislicePassbandBSSFP2019b,blazquezfrechesBOLDfMRIMouseAuditory2018}, which may result in sampling the transient phase rather than measuring the intended steady-state \gls{BOLD} contrast. Recent work by Heule et al.~\cite{heuleInvertedBOLDSignal2024} demonstrated that \gls{bSSFP}-\gls{BOLD} contrast evolves along the pulse train, with large vessels showing an inverted contrast during the early transient period (see \cref{fig:negBOLD}). However, this effect could not be observed \textit{in vivo} using the conventional Cartesian \gls{bSSFP} sequence, even with segmented acquisitions~\cite{heuleInvertedBOLDSignal2024}.

With spiral encoding and parallel imaging acceleration, whole-brain volumes can be acquired within 50 excitations ($\approx300$~ms), thereby enabling sampling of the early transient phase of \gls{bSSFP}-\gls{BOLD} contrast. Preliminary observations suggest voxels that are positively correlated in the passband steady-state experiment showing inverted responses during the transient period, as can be seen from \cref{fig:negBOLD}. Although the underlying mechanism of this contrast inversion remains unclear, increased contributions from several signal pathways during the transient period, as illustrated in \cref{fig:bSSFP_EPG}, may provide a starting point for further investigation.

\subsection{Concurrent Monitoring}
\label{subsec:concurrentMonitoring}
Gradient imperfections can be measured using $^{19}$F field probes ~\cite{barmetSpatiotemporalMagneticField2008} and modeled as a \gls{LTI} system in the reconstruction ~\cite{vannesjoCharacterizingCorrectingImperfect2013}, as detailed in \cref{sec:GIRF}. However, concurrent monitoring provides higher precision through real-time tracking of spatiotemporal field distortions. Additionally it enables the detection of physiological field fluctuations caused by motion, respiration, and cardiac cycle~\cite{grossPhysiologyRecordingMagnetic2017}. To this end, during the course of this presented work a dedicated RF array with 16 integrated field probes was developed~\cite{bauseDesignApplication16channel2024a}. The probes were positioned to maximize their sensitivity to field components up to 3\textsuperscript{rd}-order spherical harmonics, while preserving the transmit–receive performance of the RF array. A proof-of-concept \textit{in vivo} experiment demonstrated concurrent monitoring during a spiral measurement, as illustrated in \cref{fig:concurrentmonitoring}. Although the improvements compared to \gls{GIRF}-based correction were modest, the sufficiently long probe lifetimes and demonstrated physiological monitoring highlight the potential of this approach to reduce the temporal fluctuations in the functional time series and warrants further investigation.

To improve the accessibility of this method, further improvements could be made in reconstruction speed with modern heterogeneous computing architectures~\cite{stoneAcceleratingAdvancedMRI2008,florianknollGpuNUFFTOpenSource2014} and integrating them into the online reconstruction pipeline~\cite{hansenGadgetronOpenSource2013} for a workflow comparable to conventional \gls{GE}-\gls{EPI}. These directions could potentially advance the understanding and the utility of non-Cartesian \gls{bSSFP} for functional investigations.

\subsection{Melanoma Metastasis Patient Study}
\label{subsec:dmipatient}

In an ongoing feasibility study, the optimized \gls{DMI} protocols from publication~1 are being applied to patients with melanoma brain metastases to assess their ability to detect tumours. Unlike the pronounced lactate signal and reduced \gls{Glx} synthesis reported for a large glioblastoma~\cite{defeyterDeuteriumMetabolicImaging2018}, the spatial metabolite maps obtained using all methods do not reveal a distinct metabolic signature. \Gls{ROI} analysis in \cref{fig:dmipatient} shows a small lactate signal at the first time point that diminishes in the scan acquired approximately 40\,min later. Because the current resolution of the protocols ($\approx$3–4\,mL) significantly exceeds the lesion volume ($\approx$1\,mL), this can be attributed to dilution from partial-volume effects. The lactate amplitudes in \cref{fig:dmipatient} indicate that sub-millimolar sensitivity is essential for confident detection of such lesions. Moreover, the \gls{Glx} levels in the metastases appear comparable to those of the surrounding grey matter, limiting their diagnostic value. Future work could therefore focus on exploiting this early lactate dynamics and enhancing sensitivity for lactate detection through protocol optimization.

In addition, the dynamic spectroscopic imaging framework presented here can be extended to quantify glycolytic fluxes and derive metabolic parameters such as the cerebral glucose metabolic rate (CMR\textsubscript{glc}) and the \gls{TCA} cycle flux (V\textsubscript{TCA})~\cite{luQuantitativeAssessmentBrain2017,kreisMeasuringTumorGlycolytic2020}, offering a comprehensive view of oxidative and non-oxidative metabolism. These kinetic biomarkers are expected to provide greater diagnostic value than the static metabolite maps presented in this work. Another logical step would be is to apply the developed methods at widely-available lower field strengths. At these fields,the demonstrated spectral encoding with phase cycling can compensate for reduced chemical-shift dispersion, allowing shorter repetition times and faster signal averaging. Finally, advanced denoising techniques exploiting known priors, such as spatial smoothness~\cite{dabovImageDenoisingSparse2007}, temporal regularization with kinetic models~\cite{montraziDeuteriumImagingWarburg2023}, or learning more general spatio-temporal priors with machine learning algorithms~\cite{osburgPhysicsInformedDeepAutoencoder2025}, could further enhance image quality and diagnostic sensitivity.
