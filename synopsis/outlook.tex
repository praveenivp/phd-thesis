\section{Outlook}
\label{sec:outlook}
Passband-\gls{bSSFP} offers a potential alternative to traditional \gls{GE}-\gls{BOLD} \gls{fMRI} at \gls{UHF}, leveraging vessel size specificity to isolate \gls{BOLD} signals originating near small vessels such as capillaries to be closer to neuronal activity and to minimize contributions from larger vessels.  In publication 1, we have addressed a key practical limitation, the slow acquisition speed, with efficient spiral acquisition. However, \textit{in vivo} validation of the vessel size specificity is crucial for its broader acceptance. While demonstrated in simulations~\cite{baez-yanezImpactVesselSize2017} and phantom experiments with micro-spheres ~\cite{schefflerBOLDSensitivityRapid2019}, replicating these findings \textit{in vivo} remains challenging. In a recent study, the superior signal stability of \gls{bSSFP} across cortical layer compared with GE-\gls{BOLD} was demonstrated with resting-state functional experiments~\cite{ramadanMacrovascularContributionsRestingstate2025}. Building on this, we conducted two preliminary investigations to characterize the vessel-size specificity of bSSFP-BOLD contrast, briefly presented in the following the subsections \ref{subsec:bssfp_bold} and \ref{subsec:funcphasecycling}. We also explored the peculiar BOLD contrast inversion during the transient phase of bSSFP and evaluated the benefits of using concurrently measured spatio-temporal field information from a newly built integrated field-probe RF array to enhance the image quality. Looking forward, further improvements could be achieved by accelerating reconstruction with modern heterogeneous computing architectures and integrating them into the online reconstruction pipelines for a workflow comparable to conventional \gls{GE}-\gls{EPI}. These directions could potentially advance the understanding and the utility of non-Cartesian \gls{bSSFP} for functional investigations.

The improved sensitivity of the \gls{DMI} methods developed in this work holds promise for advancing cancer diagnosis and treatment monitoring. While the current study focused on healthy subjects, future investigations can explore the application of these techniques to characterize tumor metabolism. Specifically, imaging smaller tumors, such as melanoma metastases or low-grade gliomas, would demonstrate the clinical potential of high-resolution \gls{DMI}. However, an actual resolution of approximately 12.6 mm isotropic may be insufficient for reliable diagnosis in real-world scenarios. This is particularly challenging with low-grade tumors, which retain normal brain function (i.e., Glx synthesis) and exhibit reduced lactate production compared to large, aggressive gliomas exceeding (50 mm)$^3$ in size with significant lactate accumulation. Additionally, the dynamic imaging employed in this study could be used to estimate glycolytic fluxes and derive quantitative metabolic parameters, including cerebral metabolic rates of oxygen (CMR$_{O_2}$) and glucose (CMR$_{\text{Glx}}$), providing a comprehensive assessment of both oxidative and non-oxidative metabolism~\cite{luQuantitativeAssessmentBrain2017}. These metabolic biomarkers may ultimately offer greater diagnostic value than the standalone metabolite maps at different points in this study. Finally, advanced denoising techniques exploiting known priors, such as spatial smoothness, temporal regularization with kinetic models, or learning more general spatio-temporal priors with machine learning algorithms, could further enhance image quality and diagnostic sensitivity.

\subsection{Investigating large-vessel bias}
\label{subsec:veins}
In this preliminary study, \gls{BOLD} signal changes were analyzed near large vessels segmented from a co-registered high-resolution Cartesian \gls{GRE} scan. The underlying hypothesis is that bSSFP-BOLD should exhibit reduced signal changes near large veins compared to \gls{GE}-\gls{BOLD}, reflecting improved spatial specificity. As shown in \cref{fig:vesselDistance}, \gls{bSSFP}-\gls{BOLD} signal changes near large vessels are marginally lower, suggesting a degree of vessel-specific sensitivity. This effect becomes more pronounced in functional \gls{bSSFP} experiments conducted with shorter \gls{TR}, where suppression of large-vessel contributions is more clearly visible.  Although these findings are encouraging, the overall reduced \gls{BOLD}-related signal change makes it difficult to fully assess the vessel-size sensitivity.

\subsection{BOLD signal change in refocusing modes}
\label{subsec:funcphasecycling}
Demonstrating small-vessel specificity of bSSFP-BOLD in the human brain is challenging due to the difficulty of vascular measurement and segmentation. An alternative approach is to examine BOLD signal changes in the refocusing echo pathways of bSSFP, as described in \cref{sec:bssfp_conf_modes}. In our preliminary investigation ~\cite{iyyappanvalsalaInvestigationBSSFPFrequency2024}, high-resolution bSSFP frequency responses were acquired with an RF phase increment resolution of 1.8°, and the corresponding \gls{BOLD}-related signal changes were analyzed in the \gls{SSFP} configuration modes. Experimental limitations prevented fully sampled bSSFP profiles under both active and resting conditions within two functional runs, due to scanner drifts and the slow hemodynamic response. Nonetheless, a consistent difference (stopband width) between resting and active profiles was observed for highly significant activated voxels (z-score > 15) during the passband experiment, as illustrated in \cref{fig:bssfpBOLDmodes}. Refocusing SSFP modes amplitudes exhibited larger signal differences, suggesting that bSSFP-BOLD signal changes arise predominantly from refocusing pathways rather than dephasing modes for short TR/TE (5/1.6~ms).

\subsection{Transient bSSFP-BOLD}

The high acquisition speed of the 3D spiral-bSSFP sequence enabled the observation of bSSFP-BOLD contrast inversion during the transient phase. 


In passband-\gls{bSSFP} \gls{fMRI} studies, 2D multi-slice \gls{bSSFP} has been proposed to improve coverage ~\cite{reynaudMultislicePassbandBSSFP2019b,blazquezfrechesBOLDfMRIMouseAuditory2018}, which may result in sampling the transient rather than the steady-state signal. Recent work by Rahel et al.~\cite{heuleInvertedBOLDSignal2024} demonstrated that bSSFP-BOLD contrast evolves along the pulse train, with large vessels showing an inverted contrast during the early transient phase (\cref{fig:negBOLD}). However, this effect could not be observed \textit{in vivo} using conventional Cartesian encoding, even with segmented acquisitions ~\cite{heuleInvertedBOLDSignal2024}.  

With spiral encoding and parallel imaging acceleration, whole-brain volumes can be acquired within 50 excitations ($\approx 300$~ms), thereby enabling sampling of the early transient phase of bSSFP-BOLD contrast. Preliminary observations suggest that voxels positively correlated in the passband steady-state experiment showing inverted responses during the transient phase(\cref{fig:negBOLD}). Although the underlying mechanism of this contrast inversion remains unclear, increased contributions from several signal pathways during the transient phase, as illustrated in \cref{fig:bSSFP_modes}, may provide a starting point for further investigation.

\subsection{Concurrent monitoring}
Gradient imperfections can be measured using $^{19}$F field probes ~\cite{barmetSpatiotemporalMagneticField2008} and modeled as a \gls{LTI} system in the reconstruction ~\cite{vannesjoCharacterizingCorrectingImperfect2013}, as detailed in \cref{sec:GIRF}. However, concurrent monitoring provides higher precision through real-time tracking of spatiotemporal field distortions and additionally enables the detection of physiological field fluctuations caused by motion, respiration, and cardiac cycle ~\cite{grossPhysiologyRecordingMagnetic2017}. To this end, a dedicated RF array with 16 integrated field probes was developed for our 9.4~T scanner ~\cite{bauseDesignApplication16channel2024a}. The probes were positioned to maximize sensitivity to fields up to 3\textsuperscript{rd}-order spherical harmonics, while preserving the transmit–receive performance of the RF array. A proof-of-concept *in vivo* experiment demonstrated concurrent monitoring of a spiral trajectory, as illustrated in \cref{fig:concurrentmonitoring}. Although the improvements compared to \gls{GIRF}-based correction were minimal, the sufficiently long probe lifetimes and demonstrated physiological monitoring highlight the potential of this approach to reduce temporal fluctuations in functional time series.
