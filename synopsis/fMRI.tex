\section{Functional MRI}
\label{sec:fmri}
\Gls{fMRI} using the \gls{BOLD} contrast is an indispensable non-invasive method to investigate brain function and organization. In the brain, there is an intimate connection between neurons and vasculature called neurovascular coupling~\cite{iadecolaNeurovascularUnitComing2017} to meet the local and high energy demands, as shown in \cref{fig:BBB_microscopy}. Neuronal activity increases local perfusion, thereby increasing blood oxygenation, blood volume, and blood flow. This process can be detected as a relative signal increase in a dynamic measurement as the \gls{BOLD} signal~\cite{ogawaBrainMagneticResonance1990}, since oxygenated blood has a much higher $T_2^*$ than deoxygenated blood. \Gls{BOLD}-\gls{fMRI} experiments are predominantly performed using \gls{GE} sequences with a fast \gls{EPI} readout\cite{mansfieldMultiplanarImageFormation1977} at a field strength of 3 T. The spatio-temporal resolution of fMRI methods is limited due to the low intrinsic sensitivity of \gls{MRI} and the relatively small signal change of 1-5\% at 3 T~\cite{vanderzwaagFMRI1532009}. The typical spatial resolution of fMRI studies at 3 T is about 2-3 mm\cite{bollmannNewAcquisitionTechniques2021}.

Functional studies have progressively increased in complexity to investigate microscopic neuronal circuits involving individual cortical layers~\cite{lawrenceLaminarFMRIApplications2019} and columnar structures~\cite{yacoubHighfieldFMRIUnveils2008}. Historically, investigations at such spatial scales were feasible only through electrophysiological approaches employing implanted electrode arrays, restricting experimentation to animal models. Several synergistic hardware and methodological developments have expanded the capabilities of human \gls{fMRI}. Notably, \gls{UHF} MRI scanners ($\geq 7$T) now enable \gls{fMRI} investigations at sub-millimeter resolutions, as illustrated in \cref{fig:fMRI_resolution}. Much of this improvement in \gls{fMRI} sensitivity stems from the supra-linear increase in \gls{SNR}~\cite{pohmannSignaltonoiseRatioMR2016} and the significantly higher susceptibility-related $T_2^*$ contrast at high field strengths~\cite{polimeniNeuroimagingUltrahighField2018}. Additionally, other hardware advances such as high-performance gradients, RF arrays with a large number of receive elements, and temporal field monitoring systems~\cite{barmetSpatiotemporalMagneticField2008} improved the spatio-temporal resolution and image quality.
As \gls{fMRI} is a dynamic imaging technique, reduction in acquisition time through fast encoding strategies and parallel imaging described in \cref{sec:spiralacq} is a critical advancement as well.

\subsection{GE-BOLD limitations}
The blood oxygenation level-dependent (\gls{BOLD}) contrast mechanism is predominantly a vascular susceptibility effect, not a direct measure of neuronal activity~\cite{logothetisNeurophysiologicalInvestigationBasis2001}. Consequently, the intrinsic spatial resolution of \gls{BOLD} is fundamentally limited by the underlying vasculature~\cite{duvernoyCorticalBloodVessels1981}. Neuronal events are spatially blurred by susceptibility changes originating from large draining veins~\cite{barthVeryHighresolutionThreedimensional2007,markuerkiagaCorticalVascularModel2016, bauseImpactProspectiveMotion2020} and temporally blurred by the slow hemodynamic response~\cite{sieroBOLDSpecificityDynamics2013}. While advances in image acquisition can mitigate blurring and distortions from the imaging process itself, they cannot overcome these fundamental biological constraints. This often means that the improvements in nominal resolution do not directly translate to enhanced functional localization accuracy. Furthermore, at ultra-high fields, the increased susceptibility contrast exacerbates sensitivity to physiological noise~\cite{triantafyllouPhysiologicalNoiseSignaltonoise2011} and other signal variations, such as cerebral cortical orientation dependence~\cite{viessmannDependenceRestingstateFMRI2019,ramadanMacrovascularContributionsRestingstate2025}.

To narrow the gap between the observed hemodynamic signal and true neuronal events, several methods targeting the micro-vasculature have been proposed. These techniques complement \gls{UHF}-\gls{fMRI} by trading some of the high sensitivity of \gls{GE}-\gls{BOLD} for improved specificity. Among them are \gls{VASO}~\cite{luFunctionalMagneticResonance2003,huberNonBOLDContrastLaminar2019} and \gls{ASL}~\cite{borogovacArterialSpinLabeling2012}, which aim to provide finer localization by targeting \gls{CBV} and \gls{CBF} changes, respectively. In this work, we explored \gls{bSSFP} as a \gls{SE}-like technique to suppress macrovascular \gls{BOLD} contributions and improve the specificity of \gls{BOLD}-\gls{fMRI}.

To understand \gls{SE}-\gls{BOLD} contrast, one must consider the individual components of susceptibility-related signal loss caused by field gradients of the vascular network. These are characterized by two main components: 1) a large static dephasing component, dominant around large blood vessels where diffusion effects are negligible due to relatively smooth field gradients, and 2) a dynamic averaging component, dominant around small vessels where diffusion through strong field gradients is significant~\cite{weisskoffMicroscopicSusceptibilityVariation1994,boxermanMrContrastDue1995}. As spin echo (\gls{SE}) sequences refocus static dephasing, \gls{SE}-\gls{BOLD} contrast primarily reflects the latter effect. Therefore, \gls{SE}-\gls{BOLD} offers higher localization precision by being weighted towards the capillary network while suppressing signal changes from larger draining veins. To leverage this gain in specificity, several \gls{SE}-like methods have been developed, such as \gls{SE}-\gls{EPI}~\cite{yacoubSpinechoFMRIHumans2003,buddeFunctionalMRIHuman2014}, \gls{bSSFP}~\cite{bowenHighFieldBalancedSSFP2005,schefflerHighresolutionFunctionalImaging2015}, and \gls{GRASE}~\cite{kemperSubmillimeterT2Weighted2015}, among others~\cite{norrisSpinechoFMRIPoor2012}. Among these, \gls{bSSFP} is particularly well-suited for \gls{UHF}-\gls{fMRI} due to its low \gls{SAR} requirement and high temporal resolution \cite{bowenHighFieldBalancedSSFP2006,schefflerHighresolutionMappingNeuronal2016,ehsesMultilineBalancedSSFP2018}. A critical trade-off for all these \gls{SE}-like methods is their inherently lower sensitivity and typically longer acquisition duration compared to the conventional \gls{GE}-\gls{BOLD} methods.


\subsection{bSSFP BOLD}
\label{subsec:bssfp_bold}
After Ogawa et al.~\cite{ogawaBrainMagneticResonance1990} reported measuring brain function with susceptibility changes in the blood in 1990, the first \gls{bSSFP} \gls{fMRI} application was reported a decade later~\cite{schefflerDetectionBOLDChanges2001}. The initial methods~\cite{schefflerDetectionBOLDChanges2001,millerFunctionalBrainImaging2003} mainly capitalized on the frequency sensitivity of \gls{bSSFP} to detect \gls{BOLD} changes in the stopband. In 2005, Bowen et al.~\cite{bowenHighFieldBalancedSSFP2005,bowenHighFieldBalancedSSFP2006} reported the first passband \gls{bSSFP} and suggested its improved robustness and lower sensitivity to large draining veins. Although the intrinsic spin-echo-like behavior of \gls{bSSFP} was already understood in principle~\cite{schefflerTrueFISPGradientechoSpinecho2003}, its microvascular specificity required rigorous validation. This was achieved through Monte-Carlo Bloch simulations incorporating diffusion within synthetic vascular networks, complemented by phantom experiments using micro-spheres~\cite{baez-yanezImpactVesselSize2017,schefflerBOLDSensitivityRapid2019}. These studies confirmed that \gls{bSSFP} elicits a vessel-size-dependent contrast similar to that of \gls{SE}. As illustrated in \cref{fig:vesselSize}, both \gls{SE} and \gls{bSSFP} show suppressed signal changes for large vessel radii ($\ge 100\,$\text{$\mu$m}) compared to \gls{GE}. Encouraged by these findings, high-resolution \gls{fMRI} studies at \gls{UHF} were performed and identified several challenges to improving the practicality of \gls{bSSFP}-\gls{fMRI}~\cite{schefflerHighresolutionFunctionalImaging2015,schefflerHighresolutionMappingNeuronal2016}. Major challenges among them are slower acquisition speed (3--4 times slower than \gls{GE}-\gls{EPI}), reduced \gls{BOLD}-related signal change (approximately half that of \gls{GE}-\gls{BOLD} in \textit{in vivo} studies~\cite{schefflerHighresolutionMappingNeuronal2016}), and limited coverage due to the well-known off-resonance sensitivity. Furthermore, inflow effects from the fresh blood entering the imaging volume~\cite{gaoInflowEffectsFunctional2012,renvallAllThatGlitters2014} and the large intravascular signal contributions from the voxels with high blood volume fractions~\cite{baez-yanezImpactVesselSize2017} also require further investigation.

Previous work~\cite{ehsesMultilineBalancedSSFP2018} using highly-segmented \gls{EPI} and the non-Cartesian trajectories proposed in this work have essentially addressed the challenge of low acquisition speed. As a side effect of slightly increasing the \gls{TR} to accommodate an efficient readout, the \gls{BOLD}-related signal change is also improved. Since the sensitivity of functional studies depends on both the signal change and the temporal stability of the acquisition method, the high temporal stability reported for \gls{bSSFP}\cite{millerSignalNoiseCharacteristics2007} brings its overall sensitivity potentially closer to that of \gls{GE}-\gls{BOLD}. Therefore, \gls{bSSFP}-\gls{BOLD} represents a promising \gls{UHF} methodology for performing \gls{fMRI} at high resolutions, despite the remaining challenges such as intravascular signal contribution, $T_2^*$ bias, and limited coverage.
