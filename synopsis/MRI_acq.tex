\subsection{NMR signal}
The source of \gls{NMR} signal arises from the intrinsic angular momentum of the atomic nuclei also known as spin. Although the behavior of the spin is quantum mechanical in nature, classical models are mostly sufficient in the scope of this thesis except for topics such as polarization and quadrapolar relaxation. Therefore, the following sections rely mostly on the classical picture of assuming macroscopic magnetization as a "spinning tiny magnets" in an external magnetic fields which obeys some classical rotational kinematics and differential equations. The complete quantum mechanical treatment of spins and origin of \gls{NMR} signal is covered in numerous sources\cite{levittSpinDynamicsBasics}\cite{haackeMagneticResonanceImaging1999}. 
\subsection{Macroscopic Magnetization}
The individual nuclei with non-zero spin are also associated with the magnetic moment. However, these magnetic moments are aligned randomly  in the absence of external magnetic field $B_0$ resulting in no net magnetic moment. According to quantum mechanics, when the spins experience an external magnetic field $B_0$, the energy state of the spin system undergoes splitting known as the \emph{Zeeman effect} into $2s+1$ discrete energy levels, where $s$ is the spin quantum number of the nuclei. These energy levels are typically $\Delta E=\hbar\gamma B_0$ apart without other interactions\cite{levittSpinDynamicsBasics}, where $\gamma$ is the gyromagnetic ratio for the nucleus and  $\hbar$ is the reduced Planck's constant ( $1.054571817\times10^{-34}$ J.s). This energy splitting favors the spin population to be in the low energy state while the thermal energy of the system tries to equalize the population in all levels. The emerging \emph{macroscopic magnetization} $M_0$ from this population imbalance can be estimated using the \emph{Boltzmann distribution}, which describes the probability of spins occupying different energy states based on their energy and the system's temperature. At room temperature, nuclear magnetic energy levels are much smaller than the thermal energy of the system(i.e $\hbar\gamma B_0 \ll kT$, where $k$ is Boltzmann's constant ($1.38066\times10^{-23}$ J/K) and T is the temperature in Kelvin). With this assumption, the infinite series from Boltzmann distribution simplifies to an analytical expression for observable \emph{macroscopic magnetization} $M_0$ that is aligned along the $B_0$ in an \gls{NMR} experiment\cite{haackeMagneticResonanceImaging1999}.
\begin{equation}
	M_0 \approxeq \rho_0 \dfrac{s(s+1)\gamma^2\hbar^2}{3kT}B_0
	\label{eq:M0}
\end{equation}
Here $\rho_0$ is the number of spins in an unit volume. A general expression is given to accommodate both spin-$tfrac{1}{1}$ hydrogen nuclei ($^1H$)and spin-1 deuterium nuclei ($^2H$) investigated in this thesis. Additionally, the above expression partially explains the sensitivity improvement of an \gls{NMR} experiment at higher field strengths.

\subsection{Excitation and detection}
The \emph{macroscopic magnetization} $M_0$ cannot be detected in an \gls{NMR} experiment. A time varying magnetic field $B_1$, typically in the \gls{RF} frequency range, is applied orthogonal to $B_0$ field to create \emph{transverse magnetization} $M_{xy}$ which can then be detected using a detection coil. 
In most cases, $B_1$ field (\gls{RF} pulse) is applied at the \emph{Larmor frequency} $\omega_0 = \gamma B_0$ to achieve on-resonance condition ($\omega_{RF}=\omega_0$). For a \gls{RF} pulse $B_1(t)= b_1(t)\cos\omega_0 t$, the \emph{longitudinal magnetization} $M_z$ aligned along the static magnetic field $B_0$ is tipped away from the $B_0$ by an angle called flip angle $\alpha$ at any time point is given by

\begin{equation}
	\alpha(t)=\gamma\int_{t_0}^{t} b_1(t')dt'
\end{equation}

A new rotating frame of reference which rotates about $B_0$ field at Larmor frequency $\omega_0$ is usually introduced to simply analysis by offsetting the modulation of the \emph{Larmor precession}. when the \gls{RF} pulse is off-resonant, a magnetic field of $\Delta\omega/\gamma$ is encountered along the $B_0$ in the rotating frame, where $\Delta\omega=\omega_0-\omega_{RF}$. During the \gls{RF} pulse, the magnetization precess along the effective field $B_{eff}(t)$ which is the vector sum of  $\Delta\omega/\gamma$ and $B_1(t)$. If the frequency is modulated appropriately, the magnetization follow the $B_{eff}$ field to create transverse magnetization. The off-resonant \gls{RF} pulse with frequency modulation in such cases are called adiabtic pulses\cite{tannusAdiabaticPulses1997}.

\subsubsection{Bloch equation}

In matrix form, the Bloch equations can be written as:
\begin{equation}	
\frac{d}{dt}
\begin{pmatrix} 
	M_x \\
	 M_y \\
	  M_z 
	\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 
	-\frac{1}{T_2} & \gamma B_z & \gamma B_1 \cos(\omega t) \\ -\gamma B_z & -\frac{1}{T_2} & \gamma B_1 \sin(\omega t) \\ -\gamma B_1 \cos(\omega t) & -\gamma B_1 \sin(\omega t) & -\frac{1}{T_1} \end{pmatrix}
	 \begin{pmatrix} M_x \\ M_y \\ M_z 
	 \end{pmatrix} 
	 + \begin{pmatrix} 0 
	 	\\ 0 \\ \frac{M_0}{T_1} \end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}
	where $M_x$, $M_y$, and $M_z$ are the magnetization components, $\gamma$ is the gyromagnetic ratio, $B_0$ is the main magnetic field strength, $B_1$ is the amplitude of the RF field, $B_z$ is the z-component of the magnetic field, $T_1$ and $T_2$ are the longitudinal and transverse relaxation times, $M_0$ is the equilibrium magnetization, $\omega$ is the Larmor frequency, and $t$ is time.
