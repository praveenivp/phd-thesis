\section{Balanced Steady State Free Precession}
\label{sec:bSSFP}
The steady-state \gls{MRI} sequences are a sub-category of fast gradient-echo sequences where the excitation pulses are applied consecutively at a constant but much faster rate than the longitudinal and transverse relaxation times $(T_1, T_2 \ll TR)$. In addition to enabling fast imaging, the signal dependence on the previous excitations generates novel structural and functional contrast mechanisms. Depending on whether the transverse magnetization is spoiled after each repetition, the steady-state methods can be classified into steady-state incoherent and coherent methods as shown in \cref{fig:ss_seq}. The incoherent methods such as \gls{FLASH} ~\cite{haaseFLASHImagingRapid1986}/\gls{SPGR} eliminate the residual transverse magnetization before the next excitation with gradient spoiling and quadratic \gls{RF} phase increments known as \gls{RF} spoiling. Despite the first rapid steady-state method \gls{FLASH} being proposed later than existing gradient-echo \gls{EPI} ~\cite{mansfieldMultiplanarImageFormation1977} and spin-echo \gls{RARE} ~\cite{hennigRAREImagingFast1986} methods, it was quickly adopted into clinical routine because of its speed, high image quality, desirable $T_1$ contrast, and reliability.
\newline
The coherent methods do not use RF spoiling. Therefore, both longitudinal and transverse components evolve over multiple repetitions resulting in a more complicated signal dependence on flip angle, $T_1$, $T_2$ and off-resonance. By carefully adjusting the unbalanced gradient moments, the \gls{FID} part of the \gls{SSFP} signal with prominent $T_1$ weighting~\cite{oppletFISPNewFast1986} or the echo part of \gls{SSFP} signal with prominent $T_2$ weighting ~\cite{gyngellApplicationSteadystateFree1988} can be acquired. The \gls{SSFP}-\gls{FID} goes by several acronyms in the literature and by vendors such as $F_0$, $S+$, \gls{FISP} (Siemens), FAST (GE), or FFE(Philips). Similarly,  \gls{SSFP}-Echo is known by its alternative names such as $F_{-1}$, $S-$, PSIF (Siemens), CS-FAST (GE), or $T_2$-FFE (Philips). These methods are not widely used in the clinical practice except for some niche applications like multi-parametric mapping~\cite{heuleTripleEchoSteadystate2014} because of their undesirable contrast and the availability of better alternatives. Furthermore, the unbalanced gradients make these methods highly sensitive to flow and motion. There are numerous other unbalanced \gls{SSFP} methods possible with even larger unbalanced gradients such as echo-shifted \gls{GE} sequences, which have only limited application for $T_2^*$-weighted imaging due to the increased motion sensitivity and exponentially decreasing sensitivity with the increasing echo-shifting order~\cite{chungSignalFormationEchoshifted1999, schefflerPictorialDescriptionSteadystates1999}.

The \gls{bSSFP} (True\gls{FISP}, FIESTA or balanced FFE) method~\cite{carrSteadyStateFreePrecession1958, zurMotioninsensitiveSteadystateFree1990} is a special case of coherent steady-state sequence where the gradient moment is nulled before each excitation. The gradient moment nulling makes the \gls{bSSFP} acquisition highly motion insensitive and, additionally, provides the possibility to
acquire signal of all echos generated by the preceding \gls{RF} pulses simultaneously. This improves the \gls{SNR} of \gls{bSSFP} acquisition and, in addition, makes it more sensitive to additional phase accumulations due to temporal field drifts and spatial $B_0$ off-resonance. The characteristic $T_2/T_1$ contrast of \gls{bSSFP} makes it ideal for fluids, with an unmatched \gls{SNR} per unit time compared to all other sequences. With the improving scanner hardware, \gls{bSSFP} is finding increasing applications in cardiac imaging, angiography, multi-parametric mapping, and adequate spin-echo contrast replacement in some cases ~\cite{schefflerPrinciplesApplicationsBalanced2003,schiedaPracticalApplicationsBalanced2017}. Due the limited scope of this thesis, only the \gls{bSSFP} signal model is introduced in the following subsections.

\subsection{Transient phase: the transition to steady-state}
In order to measure the steady-state signal, the magnetization must be brought to the desirable equilibrium condition. Depending on relaxation times, sequence parameters, and off-resonance, several repetitions of excitation, relaxation and free precession are required for the magnetization to reach the steady-state ~\cite{bieriFundamentalsBalancedSteady2013}. Normally, the magnetization will reach steady-state after some repetitions with no signal acquisition, known as dummy cycles, for the duration of $5\cdot T_1$ ~\cite{schefflerTransientPhaseBalanced2003}. Often this long preparation is impractical. Therefore, short preparation blocks were proposed such as ($\alpha/2$ pulse - TR/2) preparation~\cite{deimingMagnetizationPreparedTrue1994}, linear flip angle ramp ~\cite{nishimuraAnalysisReductionTransient}, and optimized preparation block based on eigenvector analysis ~\cite{hargreavesCharacterizationReductionTransient2001,jaynesMatrixTreatmentNuclear1955}. A shaped flip angle ramp is usually favored for its simplicity and for having a short transient phase with strongly reduced oscillatory components due to the off-resonant magnetization.    

\subsection{BSSFP Frequency Response}
In the steady-state, a closed-form signal amplitude can be obtained by modeling the periodic nature of the magnetization~\cite{jaynesMatrixTreatmentNuclear1955,zurMotioninsensitiveSteadystateFree1990}. The steady-state transverse magnetization $M^+_{\perp}$ for flip angle $\alpha$, relaxation times $T_1$ and $T_2$, and repetition time TR is given by~\cite{ganterSteadyStateGradient2006}

\begin{equation}
M^+_{\perp}(\alpha,\theta)=M_0\frac{-i(1-E_1)\sin \alpha (1-E_2e^{-i\theta})}{(1-E_1\cos \alpha)(1-E_2\cos \theta)-(E_1-\cos \alpha)(E_2- \cos \theta)E_2},
	\label{eq:bSSFPfreqResponse}	
\end{equation}

with the relaxation terms $E_1 \coloneqq \exp\left(-\frac{\text{TR}}{T_{1}}\right)$ and  $E_{2} \coloneqq \exp\left(-\frac{\text{TR}}{T_2}\right)$ and the phase term $\theta\coloneqq \vartheta- \varphi$ being the phase accumulated by the transverse magnetization during each \gls{TR} due to off-resonance $\vartheta$ and RF phase increment $\varphi$.

The steady-state signal of \gls{bSSFP} exhibits a strong dependence on off-resonance effects, in contrast to gradient- or RF-spoiled \gls{GE} sequences. In addition to off-resonance, the shape of the bSSFP frequency response depends on both flip angle and $T_2/T_1$ ratio, as shown in \cref{fig:bSSFP_freqresp}. Notably, the magnitude part of the bSSFP frequency response is periodic with a period of 1/\gls{TR}. The regions of high signal within this response are referred to as the passband, which covers about 70\% of the overall frequency response. Conversely, the low signal regions are termed the stopband, which are responsible for banding artifacts commonly observed in bSSFP images. It is worth noting that this behavior is reversed in the low flip angle regime, where the passband and stopband characteristics change. Unlike spoiled acquisition schemes, the optimal flip angle for maximizing the passband steady-state signal in bSSFP depends on both $T_1$ and $T_2$ relaxation times~\cite{bieriFundamentalsBalancedSteady2013}. The maximum possible transverse magnetization of $0.5\,\cdot\,M_0$ is achieved when $T_1$ and $T_2$ are equal and the flip angle is $90^\circ$. Another interesting feature of the frequency response is the constant phase response for off-resonances less than 1/\gls{TR}. This behavior bears a striking similarity to the spin-echo technique, where all off-resonances exhibit zero phase at the \gls{TE}. This intriguing resemblance~\cite{schefflerTrueFISPGradientechoSpinecho2003} is used in some niche spin-echo-like applications~\cite{bowenHighFieldBalancedSSFP2005,schefflerHighresolutionFunctionalImaging2015} where acquisition speed and high energy deposition are of primary concern. It should be noted that the presented bSSFP frequency response is derived from a single isochromat. In an imaging experiment, the distribution of isochromats, as indicated by the intravoxel spectrum, introduces asymmetries~\cite{millerAsymmetriesBalancedSSFP2010} and alters the refocusing behavior of bSSFP~\cite{ganterStaticSusceptibilityEffects2006}.

\subsection{Phase Cycling}
The bSSFP imaging experiment is typically conducted under passband conditions, where a linear \gls{RF} phase increment of $\varphi = 180^\circ$ is used to keep on-resonant spins within the passband. Achieving sufficient $B_0$ shimming across the entire imaging volume to avoid banding artifacts is challenging, particularly at high field strengths. Mitigation strategies include (1) shimming only the region of interest, (2) reducing TR to increase the banding-width, and (3) adjusting the system frequency or linear RF phase increment to shift banding regions away from the region of interest. However, when a sufficiently homogeneous static field cannot be achieved, multiple bSSFP acquisitions with different linear RF phase increments can be used to improve coverage. This process is known as phase cycling. The main drawback of phase cycling is the increased acquisition time. Furthermore, the combined images have lower \gls{SNR} efficiency and may exhibit different contrast compared to passband \gls{bSSFP} acquisitions, depending on the combination method~\cite{bangerterAnalysisMultipleacquisitionSSFP2004}.
 
\subsection{SSFP Configuration Modes}
\label{subsec:bssfp_conf_modes}
The \gls{bSSFP} signal can alternatively be described in the \gls{SSFP} configuration space~\cite{vlaardingerbroekMagneticResonanceImaging2003} using the \gls{EPG} formalism~\cite{woessnerEffectsDiffusionNuclear1961,weigelExtendedPhaseGraphs2015}. This framework facilitates the analysis of \gls{bSSFP} signal characteristics, as the behavior of individual configuration modes (or echoes) is well understood. Configuration modes correspond to coherent transverse magnetization that contributes to the observable signal after undergoing multiple RF excitations, gradient-induced dephasing, and relaxation cycles~\cite{hennigEchoesHowGenerate1991}. In the \gls{EPG} notation, these modes are denoted as $F_{\pm k}$, where $k$ indicates the mode order and the sign distinguishes between dephasing ($F_{+k}$) and rephasing ($F_{-k}$) pathways. The order $k$ reflects the number of \gls{TR} periods the transverse magnetization has experienced before forming an echo. For example, $F_0$ (previously referred to as the \gls{SSFP}-\gls{FID}) represents the signal from transverse magnetization generated in the current repetition, whereas $F_{-1}$ (previously termed the \gls{SSFP}-echo) represents transverse magnetization generated in the previous repetition and partially refocused in the current repetition. More generally, each of these modes can be isolated and imaged by dedicated non-balanced sequences, in principle to an arbitrary order $k$ by introducing additional spoiler moments. Since the gradient moment is nulled in each repetition for the \gls{bSSFP} acquisition, its steady-state transverse magnetization corresponds to the summation of all echo amplitudes that would be created in isolation by these $F_k$-sequences~\cite{zurMotioninsensitiveSteadystateFree1990}, as illustrated in \cref{fig:bSSFP_echoes}.

In order to separate the contributions from individual echo pathways without introducing additional gradient moments, phase cycling can be employed as described by Zur et al.~\cite{zurMotioninsensitiveSteadystateFree1990}. The transverse magnetization immediately after the RF pulse, $M_+$, can be expressed as  
\begin{equation}
	M^+_{\perp} = \sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} A_n e^{jn\theta},
\end{equation}  

where $A_n$ denotes the $n^{\text{th}}$ echo amplitude, and $\theta$ is the total phase accumulated during each \gls{TR}. In the absence of off-resonance effects $\vartheta$, the phase of the individual echoes can be modulated solely by the linear RF phase increment. This enables isolation of the individual \gls{SSFP} mode amplitudes through interferometric techniques. When the RF phase increments are evenly spaced and span the full $360^{\circ}$ range, a simple \gls{DFT} can be applied to directly compute the echo amplitudes~\cite{zurMotioninsensitiveSteadystateFree1990}. As shown in \cref{fig:bSSFP_EPG}, the simulated \gls{bSSFP} signal in the brain at 9.4~T is dominated by lower-order \gls{SSFP} configurations, which decay approximately exponentially with increasing order $k$. Therefore, the mode amplitudes can be reliably separated with a relatively small number of acquired phase cycles.
