
For dynamic imaging like \gls{fMRI}, acquisition speed is of paramount importance. Currently, most \gls{fMRI} studies are performed with Cartesian \gls{EPI} where multiple lines of k-space is acquired after a single \gls{RF} excitation. In a conventional \gls{EPI}, k-space is sampled in a uniform Cartesian grid to have a quick and simple reconstruction. However, the sampling efficiency can be further improved by employing non-Cartesian trajectories such as spiral \cite{ahnHighSpeedSpiralScanEcho1986}. The smooth curvature of the spiral trajectories achieves faster and efficient sampling by minimizing abrupt k-space jumps and using multiple gradient axis simultaneously. Additionally, spiral imaging offers robustness against motion and flow \cite{nishimuraVelocityKSpaceAnalysis1995}. Despite these advantages, adoption of spiral imaging is slow due to the complex non-Cartesian reconstruction, system imperfections and blurring due to spatial $B_0$ homogeneity \cite{gloverSpiralImagingFMRI2012}. In this work, a 3D encoding scheme called stack-of-spirals \cite{irarrazabalFastThreeDimensional1995} is employed for its suitability with the \gls{bSSFP} acquisition(explained in \cref{sec:bSSFP}). The Cartesian phase encoding orthogonal to spiral encoding direction supports asymmetric \gls{FOV} to achieve high resolution in a thin slab and reduces reconstruction complexity.

\subsection{Trajectory Design}
The 3D stack-of-spirals trajectory is constructed by rotating and stacking many short spiral interleaves which starts from the k-space center and spirals out as shown in \cref{fig:sos_seq_traj}. A single spiral interleaf is an Archimedian spiral $k(t)$ with uniform density (spiral turns are equidistant) defined as    
\begin{equation}
	k(t)=k_x(t)+jk_y(t)=\lambda\theta e^{j\theta}
\end{equation} 
where $\lambda=N_\text{int}/\text{FOV}$, $N_\text{int}$ is the number of spiral interleaves and $\theta(t)$ is the azimuthal function.  The azimuthal function $\theta(t)$ is calculated to meet both the sampling requirement and the gradient system constraints such as maximum gradient amplitude and maximum slew rate limit. As there is no closed form expression, conventionally spiral gradient waveform is estimated either iteratively or by approximations  \cite{gloverSpiralImagingFMRI2012,duynFastSpiralMagnetic1997,gloverSimpleAnalyticSpiral1999}. Here, we used a flexible framework for gradient waveform calculation where Archimedian spiral k-space locations as per sampling requirement is provided to an optimization algorithm. The time-optimal gradient waveform for the input trajectory is estimated for a given gradient constraints \cite{lustigFastMethodDesigning2008}.

\subsection{NUFFT}
The spiral k-space sampling results in a data that doesn't lie on a equidistant Cartesian grid preventing the use of efficient \gls{FFT} algorithm for image reconstruction. To compute Fourier transform of the data sampled on a non-equidistant grid, several efficient \gls{NUFFT} algorithms exists \cite{duttFastFourierTransforms1993} were introduced. The most popular among them is the gridding method where a shift-invariant interpolating kernel is used to regrid the non-equidistant data into an oversampled Cartesian grid followed by efficient \gls{FFT} and removing the blurring introduced by the interpolation kernel through deapodization \cite{jacksonSelectionConvolutionFunction1991}. This use of same shift-invariant kernel throughout the k-space reduces the memory requirement of gridding operation. Ideally, \emph{sinc} kernel gives the exact \gls{DFT} and needs no further no deapodization. However, the large support of \emph{sinc} kernel makes it computationally inefficient. Therefore, trade-off between approximation error and computational complexity of the interpolating kernel has to be reached. The \emph{Kaiser-Bessel} function is a popular choice because of its small approximation error for a relatively small kernel width and oversampling ratio \cite{beattyRapidGriddingReconstruction2005}. The approximation error can be further minimized if an ideal interpolating kernel is estimated from the trajectory. For this reason, the min-max interpolator \cite{fesslerNonuniformFastFourier2003} and its efficient implementation as \emph{Toplitz} matrix \cite{fesslerToeplitzbasedIterativeImage2005} is used in this work.
\subsection{Density compensation}
\label{sec:DCF}
In addition to \gls{NUFFT}, the sampling density of the trajectory must be compensated to avoid accentuation of some spatial frequencies which are densely sampled. Generally, the densely-sampled low spatial frequencies during the slew-rate limited regime of the spiral trajectory resulting in blurring. When spiral arms have only a few windings, a simple \emph{rho filter} used for the filtered back-projection reconstruction of computed-tomography and radial MR imaging could be used for density compensation. However, density compensation function become non-linear as trajectory transitions into amplitude restricted regime. Among the several numerical methods proposed for density compensation \cite{hogeDensityCompensationFunctions1997,pipeSamplingDensityCompensation1999,malikGriddingAlgorithmEfficient2005}, the reciprocal of \emph{area density function} introduced by Jackson et al. \cite{jacksonSelectionConvolutionFunction1991} is used in this work.      

\subsection{Gradient Impulse Response Function}
\label{sec:GIRF}
The fidelity of gradient system is limited by eddy current effects, mechanical resonances, coil coupling, temperature and amplifier bandwidth. The resulting inconsistencies in the trajectory information result in undesired image blurring, distortions, rotation and translation. To improve the image quality, the actual trajectory information from a direct measurement \cite{barmetSpatiotemporalMagneticField2008,vannesjoImageReconstructionUsing2016} or a prediction model \cite{vannesjoGradientSystemCharacterization2013} should be included into the reconstruction framework. Owing to the technological challenges of concurrent monitoring of the gradient fields, a \gls{LTI} model of the gradient system known as \gls{GIRF} is used for trajectory prediction. The \gls{GIRF} is determined by observing the response of the system for the known inputs. Typically, triangular pulses of different durations are applied to a the individual gradient axis and the resulting spatio-temporal field evolution are measured with a phantom \cite{rahmerRapidAcquisition3D2019a} or a dedicated field camera setup \cite{vannesjoGradientSystemCharacterization2013}.  The long lifetimes ($T_2^* \approx$ 50 ms) of the dedicated field probe system yields a \gls{GIRF} with much higher spectral resolution than an equivalent phantom experiment.  Multiple triangular pulses are used to probe the \gls{GIRF} in all frequencies without any blind spots as shown in \cref{fig:GIRF}. Alternatively, a frequency sweeping chirp waveform can be used for rapid calibration \cite{kronthalerTrajectoryCorrectionBased2021}.
\begin{figure}[H]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=1.0\textwidth]{graphics/GIRF.png}
	\caption{Sensitivity of gradient impulse response function (GIRF) measurement. The input triangular gradient waveform of varying duration (left) and their frequency response (right). The blue dotted line represents the overall sensitivity from all the triangular pulses which does not exhibit the zero crossings of individual frequency response.}
	\label{fig:GIRF}
\end{figure}

If the input triangular pulses in Fourier domain $I_j(\omega)$ yield the system responses $\hat{O}_j(\omega)$, then the \gls{GIRF} $\hat{H}(\omega)$ can be calculated according to Vannesjo et al. \cite{vannesjoGradientSystemCharacterization2013}

\begin{equation}
	\label{eq:GIRF}
	\hat{H}(\omega)=\frac{\sum_{j}I^*_j(\omega)\cdot\hat{O}_j(\omega)}{\sum_{j}|I_j(\omega)|^2}
\end{equation}

From \cref{eq:GIRF}, it can be inferred that multiple averages are required to improve the accuracy of the estimated \gls{GIRF} $\hat{H}(\omega)$ especially at high frequencies. The measured \gls{GIRF} for all gradient axes in the SC72CD gradient system is presented in \cref{fig:GIRF_sc72}. The magnitude response show the expected low pass filter behavior of the gradient system. The higher spectral resolution of the acquired \gls{GIRF} was sufficient to resolve the mechanical resonances of the gradient system. The \gls{GIRF} predictions of any arbitrary input gradients is obtained by frequency-domain multiplication of the nominal input gradient waveform and the measured \gls{GIRF}. The temporal extent of both the input waveform and the measured \gls{GIRF} is matched with bilateral zero-padding. The \gls{GIRF} predicted trajectories were obtained with the time integration of the inverse \gls{DFT} of the output spectrum according to \cref{eq:kspace}.

\subsection{Spiral Deblurring}
\label{sec:deblur}
The presence of additional spatio-temporal fields other than the linear encoding gradients during the signal acquisition results in image artifacts. Depending on the trajectory, the additional phase accrual due to the perturbing field emerge as different image artifacts. Similar to \gls{GIRF}, the response of the imaging system to a single-point signal called \gls{PSF} can be studied to predict the nature of image artifacts due to static off-resonance. The off-resonance effects on the corresponding PSF for different Cartesian and non-Cartesian trajectories are shown  in \cref{fig:spiral_psf}. The extend of image blurring in the non-Cartesian images depends both the strength of off-resonance and the readout length. 

\begin{figure}[H]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=1.0\textwidth]{graphics/spiral_psf.png}
	\caption{The Influence of $B_0$ off-resonance on the PSF. A) k-space showing the phase accrual during the readout for different trajectories in the presence of perturbing field. B) The \gls{PSF} in the absence of any additional perturbing field. C) The \gls{PSF} when an additional $2\pi$ phase accrual occurs due to spatial $B_0$ inhomogeneity. For conventional Cartesian trajectories, the resulting \gls{PSF} suggests a simple pixel shift, leading to minor geometric distortion in the reconstructed image. In contrast, spatial $B_0$ inhomogeneity causes significant image distortion and Nyquist ghosting in \gls{EPI} with long readouts. In case of spiral and radial trajectories, the spatial off-resonance primarily results in image blurring. }
	\label{fig:spiral_psf}
\end{figure}

This artifacts can be mitigated by modeling the off-resonance effects into the reconstruction known as conjugate phase reconstruction \cite{schombergOffresonanceCorrectionMR1999}. The straight-forward deblurring algorithm is to full encoding matrix formulation  \cite{pruessmannEncodingReconstructionParallel2006}. However, this is not practical for 3D imaging because of the large memory and computational requirements. With the assumption of the local spatial invariance of the off-resonance effects due to the smooth $B_0$ off-resonance maps, several efficient deblurring algorithms were developed \cite{schombergOffresonanceCorrectionMR1999}. The $B_0$ term ($e^{j\Delta\omega(\textbf{r}) t}$) in the reconstruction model varies with both time $t$ and position $\textbf{r}$. Therefore, the $B_0$ term is segmented into $\textbf{L}$ partitions either in time \cite{nollHomodyneDetectionMagnetic1991,suttonFastIterativeImage2003} or frequency \cite{manMultifrequencyInterpolationFast1997} and efficiently computed with the NUFFT as follows: 

\begin{equation}
	e^{j\Delta\omega(\textbf{r}) t} \approx \sum_{i=0}^{L} c_i(\Delta\omega(\textbf{r}))e^{j\Delta\omega_it} \approx \sum_{i=0}^{L} c_i(t)e^{j\Delta\omega(\textbf{r})\tau_i}
	\label{eq:B0segmentation}
\end{equation}

The $\textbf{L}$ images reconstructed with different demodulation are then combined with the interpolation weights $c_i(t)$ and $c_i(\Delta\omega(\textbf{r}))$ computed with the same \cref{eq:B0segmentation} by least squares. The number of time/frequency segments depend on the largest off-resonance frequency $\Delta\omega_{\text{max}}$ and total readout time $T$ is given by 

\begin{equation}
	L\geq \dfrac{\Delta\omega_{\text{max}}T}{\pi}
	\label{eq:levels}
\end{equation}

This requires an explicit spatial off-resonance map estimated from a separate multi-echo acquisition. The accurate $B_0$ maps require multiple post-processing steps such as spatial \cite{abdul-rahmanFastRobustThreedimensional2007} or temporal \cite{robinsonMethodUnwrappingHighly2014} phase unwrapping, masking and suppression of large erroneous off-resonance values outside the object with regularized estimators \cite{fesslerRegularizedFieldmapEstimation2006}. Alternatively, automatic deblurring algorithm can be formulated without an explicit B0 maps by minimizing the imaginary part of the composite image \cite{nollDeblurringNon2DFourier1992}, utilizing implicit $B_0$ encoding by RF-array cite{patzigReconstructionStrategiesMRI2022,patzigOffResonanceSelfCorrectionImplicit2021} or auto-focus with block processing \cite{smithAutomaticOffresonanceCorrection2013}.    

\subsection{Non-Cartesian Parallel imaging}\label{sec:PI}
In addition to using efficient encoding schemes with the gradient system, imaging can be accelerated further by the receive arrays. Most modern RF coils are equipped with multiple small surface coils that improve \gls{SNR} by being closer to the object \cite{roemerNMRPhasedArray1990} and enable spatial encoding with the localized sensitivity of each element \cite{sodicksonSpatialEncodingUsing2007}. Undersampling the k-space below the Nyquist rate and removing the aliasing with the encoding information from the receive arrays is called parallel imaging. Arguably, the most popular reconstruction techniques for under-sampled data are \gls{SENSE} \cite{pruessmannSENSESensitivityEncoding1999} and \gls{GRAPPA} \cite{griswoldGeneralizedAutocalibratingPartially2002}. Due to the non-equidistant k-space sampling, a more general \gls{SENSE} formulation of reconstruction problem as a linear system of equations with explicit coil sensitivity maps is adopted in this work. High-quality 3D coil sensitivity maps can be obtained with the ESPIRiT approach \cite{ueckerESPIRiTanEigenvalueApproach2014}. The self-calibrating approaches such as \gls{GRAPPA} is challenging with non-Cartesian sampling because one can not calibrate and use a single shift-invariant kernel  \cite{heidemannDirectParallelImage2006,huangSelfcalibrationMethodRadial2007}. 
\\
In regard to the undersampling strategy, single-shot spiral trajectories must be designed with a reduced \gls{FOV}. This is in contrast to the Cartesian sampling, in which some time-consuming phase-encoding steps are omitted. In our case, the short \gls{TR} of \gls{bSSFP} acquisition warrants multiple short spiral readouts known as interleaves. Consequently, some interleaves are skipped depending on the acceleration factor. Unlike Cartesian undersampling, the aliasing due to undersampling is not coherent and affects the entire image in case of non-Cartesian spiral trajectory as shown in \cref{fig:undersampling}. This incoherent alias pattern has been demonstrated to be advantageous in achieving higher acceleration factors compared to regular Cartesian undersampling by minimizing spatially varying noise amplification called g-factor \cite{pruessmannSENSESensitivityEncoding1999,eggersNoiseAmplificationNonCartesian,leeSignaltonoiseRatioBenefit2021}. Intuitively, the additional g-factor noise is attributed to the ill-conditioning of the reconstruction problem in the regions where coil sensitivity remains constant across coil elements. This typically occurs in the center of the coil and at higher acceleration factors, where multiple aliases are present. Given that virtually all voxels alias with each other during spiral undersampling, the system of linear equations formed with all voxels and their corresponding coil sensitivities must be solved together. This is untenable for 3D imaging with large matrix sizes because of the limited computer memory. Therefore, with the exception of 2D case, the reconstruction problem is most often solved iteratively \cite{pruessmannAdvancesSensitivityEncoding2001}. The individual iteration is speed up by using efficient forward and adjoint operators formulated with the trajectory corrected \gls{NUFFT} and segmented $B_0$ off-resonance terms explained in \cref{sec:deblur}. The number of iterations required for convergence is minimized by using the conjugate gradient algorithm and pre-conditioning of the \gls{NUFFT} with the computed \gls{DCF} \cite{pruessmannAdvancesSensitivityEncoding2001} discussed in \cref{sec:DCF}.  Recent advances in constrained reconstruction, such as compress sensing \cite{lustigSparseMRI} and learned priors with artificial neural networks \cite{knollDeepLearningMethodsParallel2020}, hold considerable promise for further image acceleration possibilities. However, due to the unknown influence of these techniques on the underlying sparse BOLD signal, they are not utilized in this study.
