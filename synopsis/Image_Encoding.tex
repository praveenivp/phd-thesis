\section{Spiral Acquisition}
\label{sec:spiralacq}
For dynamic imaging like \gls{fMRI}, acquisition speed is of paramount importance. Currently, most \gls{fMRI} studies are performed with Cartesian \gls{EPI}, where multiple lines of k-space are acquired after a single \gls{RF} excitation. In conventional \gls{EPI}, k-space is sampled in a uniform Cartesian grid to have a quick and simple reconstruction. However, the sampling efficiency can be further improved by employing non-Cartesian trajectories such as spiral ~\cite{ahnHighSpeedSpiralScanEcho1986}. Compared to Cartesian \gls{EPI}, the smooth curvature of the spiral trajectories achieves faster and more efficient sampling by minimizing abrupt k-space jumps and making full use of multiple gradient axes. Additionally, spiral imaging offers robustness against motion and flow ~\cite{nishimuraVelocityKSpaceAnalysis1995}. Despite these advantages, the adoption of spiral imaging is slow due to the required complex non-Cartesian reconstruction, system imperfections and additional blurring due to spatial $B_0$ homogeneity ~\cite{gloverSpiralImagingFMRI2012}. In this work, a 3D encoding scheme called stack-of-spirals ~\cite{irarrazabalFastThreeDimensional1995} is investigated with regard of its suitability for \gls{bSSFP} acquisitions (explained in \cref{sec:bSSFP}). The employed Cartesian phase encoding orthogonal to the spiral encoding direction supports an asymmetric \gls{FOV} to achieve high resolution in a thin slab, and reduces reconstruction complexity.

\subsection{Trajectory Design}
The 3D stack-of-spirals trajectory is constructed by rotating and stacking many short spiral interleaves, which start from the k-space center and spiral out as shown in \cref{fig:sos_seq_traj}. A single spiral interleaf is an Archimedian spiral $k(t)$ with uniform density (spiral turns are equidistant) defined as    
\begin{equation}
	k(t)=k_x(t)+jk_y(t)=\lambda\theta(t) e^{j\theta(t)},
\end{equation} 
where $\lambda=N_\text{int}/\text{FOV}$, $N_\text{int}$ is the number of spiral interleaves and $\theta(t)$ is the azimuthal function.  The azimuthal function $\theta(t)$ is calculated to meet both the sampling requirement and the gradient system constraints such as maximum gradient amplitude and maximum slew rate limit. As there is no closed form expression for such $\theta(t)$, conventionally, spiral gradient waveforms are estimated either iteratively or by approximations  ~\cite{gloverSpiralImagingFMRI2012,duynFastSpiralMagnetic1997,gloverSimpleAnalyticSpiral1999}. Here, we used a flexible framework for gradient waveform calculation, where Archimedian spiral k-space locations that satisfy the sampling requirement are provided to an optimization algorithm. The time-optimal gradient waveform for the input trajectory is then estimated for given gradient constraints by the algorithm~\cite{lustigFastMethodDesigning2008}.

\subsection{NUFFT}
The spiral k-space sampling results in data that do not lie on an equidistant Cartesian grid, preventing the use of the efficient \gls{FFT} algorithm for image reconstruction. To compute the Fourier transform of the data sampled on such a non-Cartesian grid, several efficient \gls{NUFFT} algorithms were introduced~\cite{duttFastFourierTransforms1993}. The most popular among them is the gridding method, where a shift-invariant interpolating kernel is used to regrid the non-equidistant data into an oversampled Cartesian grid, followed by an efficient \gls{FFT} and subsequently removing the blurring introduced by the interpolation kernel through deapodization ~\cite{jacksonSelectionConvolutionFunction1991}. The use of the same shift-invariant kernel throughout the k-space reduces the memory requirement of the gridding operation. Ideally, a \emph{sinc} kernel gives the exact \gls{DFT} and needs no further deapodization. However, the large support of the \emph{sinc} kernel makes it computationally inefficient. Therefore, a trade-off between the approximation error and the computational complexity of the interpolating kernel has to be found. The \emph{Kaiser-Bessel} function is a popular choice because of its small approximation error for a relatively small kernel width and oversampling ratio requirements ~\cite{beattyRapidGriddingReconstruction2005}. The approximation error can be further minimized if an ideal interpolating kernel is estimated from the trajectory. For this reason, the min-max interpolator~\cite{fesslerNonuniformFastFourier2003} and its efficient implementation as \emph{Toplitz} matrix~\cite{fesslerToeplitzbasedIterativeImage2005} is used in this work.

\subsection{Density compensation}
\label{sec:DCF}
In addition to the \gls{NUFFT}, the sampling density of the trajectory must be compensated to avoid accentuation of spatial frequencies that are densely sampled. Generally, the densely-sampled low spatial frequencies during the slew-rate limited regime of the spiral trajectory result in blurring. When the spiral arms have only a few windings, a simple \emph{rho filter} used for the filtered back-projection reconstruction of computed-tomography and radial MR imaging could be used for density compensation~\cite{matsuoThreedimensionalImageReconstruction1993}. However, the density compensation function becomes non-linear as the trajectory transitions into an amplitude-restricted regime. Among the several numerical methods proposed for density compensation ~\cite{hogeDensityCompensationFunctions1997,pipeSamplingDensityCompensation1999,malikGriddingAlgorithmEfficient2005}, the reciprocal of the \emph{area density function} introduced by Jackson et al. ~\cite{jacksonSelectionConvolutionFunction1991} is used in this work.      

\subsection{Gradient Impulse Response Function}
\label{sec:GIRF}
The fidelity of the gradient system is limited by eddy current effects, mechanical resonances, coil coupling, temperature and amplifier bandwidth. The resulting inconsistencies in the trajectory information result in undesired image blurring, distortions, rotation and translation. To improve the image quality, the actual trajectory information from a direct measurement ~\cite{barmetSpatiotemporalMagneticField2008,vannesjoImageReconstructionUsing2016} or a prediction model ~\cite{vannesjoGradientSystemCharacterization2013} can be included in the reconstruction framework. Due to the technological challenges of concurrent monitoring of the gradient fields, a \gls{LTI} model of the gradient system known as \gls{GIRF} is used for trajectory prediction. The \gls{GIRF} is determined by observing the response of the system for known inputs. Typically, triangular pulses of different durations are applied to individual gradient axes and the resulting spatio-temporal field evolution is measured with a phantom ~\cite{rahmerRapidAcquisition3D2019a} or a dedicated field camera setup ~\cite{vannesjoGradientSystemCharacterization2013}.  The long lifetimes ($T_2^* \approx$ 50 ms) of the dedicated field probe system yields a \gls{GIRF} with much higher spectral resolution than an equivalent phantom experiment.  Multiple triangular pulses are used to probe the \gls{GIRF} in all frequencies without any blind spots as shown in \cref{fig:GIRF}. Alternatively, a frequency sweeping chirp waveform can be used for rapid calibration ~\cite{kronthalerTrajectoryCorrectionBased2021}.
\begin{figure}[H]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=1.0\textwidth]{graphics/GIRF_Sens.png}
	\caption{Sensitivity of a gradient impulse response function (GIRF) measurement. \textbf{(A)} The input triangular gradient waveforms of varying duration and \textbf{(B)} their frequency response. The purple dotted line represents the overall sensitivity from all the triangular pulses, which does not exhibit zero crossings of the individual frequency responses.}
	\label{fig:GIRF}
\end{figure}

If the input triangular pulses in Fourier domain $I_j(\omega)$ yield the system responses $\hat{O}_j(\omega)$, then the \gls{GIRF} $\hat{H}(\omega)$ can be calculated according to Vannesjo et al. ~\cite{vannesjoGradientSystemCharacterization2013} as

\begin{equation}
	\label{eq:GIRF}
	\hat{H}(\omega)=\frac{\sum_{j}I^*_j(\omega)\cdot\hat{O}_j(\omega)}{\sum_{j}|I_j(\omega)|^2}.
\end{equation}

From \cref{eq:GIRF}, it can be inferred that multiple averages are required to improve the accuracy of the estimated \gls{GIRF} $\hat{H}(\omega)$ especially at high frequencies. An example \gls{GIRF} measurement for all three gradient axes in the SC72CD gradient system of the Siemens MAGNETOM 9.4 T scanner, acquired using a field camera, is shown in \cref{fig:GIRF_sc72}. The magnitude response $\lvert H(\omega)\rvert$ determines the gain applied to each frequency component of the input waveform. The group delay, defined as the negative derivative of the phase response $\angle H(\omega)$, predicts the temporal delay experienced by each frequency component of the input waveform. In this case, the relatively flat, unity magnitude response and the small, constant phase slope up to 10 kHz indicate good system fidelity. The spectral resolution of the \gls{GIRF} measurement was sufficient to resolve mechanical resonances of the gradient system at approximately 590 Hz and 1140 Hz. The \gls{GIRF} predictions of any arbitrary input gradients is obtained by frequency-domain multiplication of the nominal input gradient waveform and the measured \gls{GIRF}. The temporal extent of both the input waveform and the measured \gls{GIRF} is matched with bilateral zero-padding. The \gls{GIRF}-predicted k-space trajectories were obtained by time integration of the inverse \gls{DFT} of the output spectrum according to \cref{eq:kspace}.

\subsection{Spiral Deblurring}
\label{sec:deblur}
The presence of additional spatio-temporal fields other than the linear encoding gradients during the signal acquisition results in image artifacts. Depending on the trajectory, the additional phase accrual due to the perturbing field emerges as different image artifacts. Similar to the \gls{GIRF}, the response of the imaging system to a single point-source signal is called \gls{PSF}, which can be studied to predict image artifacts due to static off-resonance. The off-resonance effects on the corresponding PSF for different Cartesian and non-Cartesian trajectories are shown  in \cref{fig:spiral_psf}. The extent of image blurring in the non-Cartesian images depends on both the strength of the off-resonance and the readout length. 

\begin{figure}[H]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=1.0\textwidth]{graphics/spiral_psf.png}
	\caption{The influence of $B_0$ off-resonance on the PSF. \textbf{(A)} k-space showing the phase accrual during the readout for different trajectories in the presence of off-resonance. \textbf{(B)} The \gls{PSF} in the absence of any additional perturbing field, which is determined by the encoding properties of the k-space trajectory as defined by the linear gradients. \textbf{(C)} The \gls{PSF} when an additional $2\pi$ phase accrual occurs due to spatial $B_0$ inhomogeneity. For conventional Cartesian trajectories, the resulting \gls{PSF} suggests a simple pixel shift, leading to minor geometric distortion in the reconstructed image. In contrast, spatial $B_0$ inhomogeneity causes significant image distortion and Nyquist ghosting in \gls{EPI} with long readouts. In case of spiral and radial trajectories, the spatial off-resonance primarily results in image blurring. }
	\label{fig:spiral_psf}
\end{figure}

These artifacts can be mitigated by modeling the off-resonance effects in the reconstruction, which is known as conjugate phase reconstruction ~\cite{schombergOffresonanceCorrectionMR1999}. The straight-forward deblurring algorithm is to use a full encoding matrix formulation~\cite{pruessmannEncodingReconstructionParallel2006}. However, this is not practical for 3D imaging because of the large memory and computational requirements. With the assumption of the local spatial invariance of the off-resonance effects due to the spatially smooth $B_0$ off-resonance maps, several efficient deblurring algorithms have been developed ~\cite{schombergOffresonanceCorrectionMR1999}. The $B_0$ term ($e^{j\Delta\omega(\textbf{r}) t}$) in the reconstruction model varies with both time $t$ and position $\textbf{r}$. Therefore, it is segmented into $L$ partitions either in time ~\cite{nollHomodyneDetectionMagnetic1991,suttonFastIterativeImage2003} or frequency ~\cite{manMultifrequencyInterpolationFast1997} and efficiently computed with the NUFFT as follows: 

\begin{equation}
	e^{j\Delta\omega(\textbf{r}) t}  \approx \sum_{i=0}^{L} c_i(t)e^{j\Delta\omega(\textbf{r})\tau_i} \approx \sum_{i=0}^{L} c_i(\Delta\omega(\textbf{r}))e^{j\Delta\omega_it}.
	\label{eq:B0segmentation}
\end{equation}

The $L$ images reconstructed with different demodulation are then combined with the interpolation weights $c_i(t)$ or $c_i(\Delta\omega(\textbf{r}))$ computed with the same \cref{eq:B0segmentation} by least squares. The required number of time or frequency segments depend on the largest off-resonance frequency $\Delta\omega_{\text{max}}$ and the total readout time $T$ as given by 

\begin{equation}
	L\geq \dfrac{\Delta\omega_{\text{max}}T}{\pi}.
	\label{eq:levels}
\end{equation}

This requires an explicit spatial off-resonance map estimated from a separate multi-echo acquisition. Obtaining accurate $B_0$ maps require multiple post-processing steps such as spatial ~\cite{abdul-rahmanFastRobustThreedimensional2007} or temporal ~\cite{robinsonMethodUnwrappingHighly2014} phase unwrapping, masking and suppression of large erroneous off-resonance values outside the object by using a regularized estimator~\cite{fesslerRegularizedFieldmapEstimation2006}. Alternatively, automatic deblurring algorithms can be formulated without an explicit $B_0$ map by minimizing the imaginary part of the composite image ~\cite{nollDeblurringNon2DFourier1992}, utilizing implicit $B_0$ encoding by a receive array~\cite{patzigReconstructionStrategiesMRI2022,patzigOffResonanceSelfCorrectionImplicit2021} or other auto-focusing approaches~\cite{smithAutomaticOffresonanceCorrection2013}. In addition to the off-resonance effects, the additional signal modulation due to $T_2^*$ relaxation also contributes to image blurring, which is not retrospectively corrected due to the unknown underlying $T_2^*$ map.

\subsection{Non-Cartesian Parallel Imaging}\label{sec:PI}
In addition to using efficient encoding schemes with the gradient system, imaging can be accelerated further by the receive arrays. Most modern \gls{RF} arrays are equipped with multiple small surface coils that improve the \gls{SNR} by being closer to the object ~\cite{roemerNMRPhasedArray1990} and enable spatial encoding with the localized sensitivity of each element ~\cite{sodicksonSpatialEncodingUsing2007}. Undersampling the k-space below the Nyquist rate and removing the resulting aliasing with the encoding information from the receive arrays is called parallel imaging. Arguably, the most popular reconstruction techniques for under-sampled data are \gls{SENSE} ~\cite{pruessmannSENSESensitivityEncoding1999} and \gls{GRAPPA} ~\cite{griswoldGeneralizedAutocalibratingPartially2002}. Due to the non-equidistant k-space sampling used in this work, a more general \gls{SENSE} formulation of the reconstruction problem as a linear system of equations with explicit coil sensitivity maps is adopted here. High-quality 3D coil sensitivity maps can be obtained with the ESPIRiT approach ~\cite{ueckerESPIRiTanEigenvalueApproach2014}. Self-calibrating approaches such as \gls{GRAPPA} are challenging with non-Cartesian sampling because one can not calibrate and use a single shift-invariant kernel  ~\cite{heidemannDirectParallelImage2006,huangSelfcalibrationMethodRadial2007}. 
\\
Regarding the undersampling strategy for parallel imaging in case of spiral trajectories, single-shot spiral must be designed with a reduced \gls{FOV}. This is in contrast to the Cartesian sampling, in which some time-consuming phase-encoding steps are omitted. In our case, the short \gls{TR} of \gls{bSSFP} acquisitions warrants multiple short spiral readouts known as interleaves. Consequently, some interleaves can be skipped depending on the acceleration factor. Unlike Cartesian undersampling, the aliasing due to undersampling in case of non-Cartesian spiral trajectories is not coherent and affects the entire image as shown in \cref{fig:undersampling}. This incoherent aliasing pattern has been demonstrated to be advantageous in achieving higher acceleration factors compared to regular Cartesian undersampling by minimizing spatially varying noise amplification quantified by the so-called g-factor ~\cite{pruessmannSENSESensitivityEncoding1999,eggersNoiseAmplificationNonCartesian2005,leeSignaltonoiseRatioBenefit2021}. Intuitively, the additional g-factor noise is attributed to the ill-conditioning of the reconstruction problem in the regions where the coil sensitivities of aliased voxels are too similar. This typically occurs in the center of the coil and at higher acceleration factors, where multiple aliases are present. Given that virtually all voxels alias with each other in case of spiral undersampling, the system of linear equations formed with all voxels and their corresponding coil sensitivities must be solved together. This is untenable for 3D imaging with large matrix sizes because of the limited computer memory. Therefore, with the exception of 2D cases, the reconstruction problem is most often solved iteratively ~\cite{pruessmannAdvancesSensitivityEncoding2001}. The individual iterations are sped up by using efficient forward and adjoint operators formulated with the trajectory-corrected \gls{NUFFT} and segmented $B_0$ off-resonance terms explained in \cref{sec:deblur}. The number of iterations required for convergence is minimized by using the conjugate gradient algorithm and pre-conditioning of the \gls{NUFFT} with the computed \gls{DCF} ~\cite{pruessmannAdvancesSensitivityEncoding2001} discussed in \cref{sec:DCF}.  Recent advances in non-linear reconstruction, such as compressed sensing ~\cite{lustigSparseMRIApplication2007} and learned priors with artificial neural networks ~\cite{hammernikLearningVariationalNetwork2018,knollDeepLearningMethodsParallel2020}, hold considerable promise for further image acceleration possibilities. However, due to the unknown influence of these techniques on the underlying sparse BOLD signal, they are not utilized in this study.
