\section{Deuterium Metabolic Imaging}
\label{sec:DMI}
\Gls{DMI} is an MR-based spectroscopic imaging technique primarily used to study metabolism. It tracks deuterium ($^2$H), a stable, non-radioactive isotope of hydrogen, within labeled substrates and detects its transformations through changes in chemical shifts. Due to its low gyromagnetic ratio (approximately 6.5 times lower than that of $^1$H) and low natural abundance (0.0156\%), only the deuterated water signal, typically around 10 mM in the brain, is readily detectable. This characteristic makes \gls{DMI} specific and robust when using exogenous contrast agents, minimizing confounding signals from endogenous sources. Furthermore, the efficient quadrupolar relaxation of the spin-1 deuterium nucleus results in short $T_1$ relaxation times (70-370 ms for detectable $^2$H metabolites at 9.4 T), enabling rapid signal averaging. \Gls{DMI} investigations are commonly performed using $[6,6^2-^2H_2]$-Glucose in conjunction with \gls{CSI}\cite{brownNMRChemicalShift1982} to acquire localized deuterium spectra. Deuterated glucose is metabolized normally within cells, producing downstream deuterated metabolites like glutamate/glutamine and lactate. By tracking these downstream metabolites, \gls{DMI} can measure glycolytic fluxes to comprehensively characterize cellular metabolism\cite{luQuantitativeAssessmentBrain2017}, going beyond the glucose uptake information provided by routinely used $[^{18}$F]fluorodeoxyglucose(FDG)- \gls{PET} in clinics. This provides additional information about oxidative and non-oxidative metabolism, which is particularly valuable in neurooncology for diagnosis and treatment monitoring, especially given the high glucose uptake of normal brain tissue.

\subsection{Tumor Biomarker}
All cells require energy in the form of adenosine triphosphate (ATP) to maintain cellular function and integrity, primarily obtained through glucose metabolism. Glucose metabolism begins with glucose transport from the blood into the cell via specialized membrane proteins, followed by glycolysis in the cytoplasm to produce pyruvate. In healthy cells, pyruvate enters the tricarboxylic acid (TCA) cycle and undergoes oxidative phosphorylation in the mitochondria for efficient ATP production, as illustrated in \cref{fig:dmi_intro}. However, tumor cells often upregulate anaerobic fermentation of pyruvate into lactic acid. Although less efficient, this process is 10–100 times faster than mitochondrial respiration and provides biomaterials for rapid cell division\cite{libertiWarburgEffectHow2016}. This results in increased glucose consumption by tumor cells, a phenomenon known as the Warburg effect\cite{warburgOriginCancerCells1956}. The Warburg effect is a key cancer biomarker utilized in various molecular imaging modalities, including FDG-\gls{PET}~\cite{reivich18FfluorodeoxyglucoseMethodMeasurement1979}, \gls{DMI}~\cite{defeyterDeuteriumMetabolicImaging2021}, hyper-polarized $^{13}C$- \gls{MRI}~\cite{gordonTranslationCarbon13EPI2019}, and Gluco\gls{CEST}~\cite{capozzaGlucoCESTMRIEvaluation2022}. Currently, the initial diagnosis of aggressive brain tumors with substantial \gls{BBB} damage relies on contrast-enhanced \gls{MRI} and \gls{CT}~\cite{herholzBrainTumors2012}. Molecular imaging techniques are important for low-grade tumor diagnosis, tumor grading for therapy planning, treatment monitoring, and recurrence detection.

\subsection{Sensitivity of DMI}
Despite the aforementioned potential of \gls{DMI}, a major drawback compared with standard FDG-\gls{PET} is its lower sensitivity. As shown in \cref{tab:proton_deuterium_comparison}, detecting deuterium metabolites at a typical 1-3 mM concentrations in the brain is approximately half-a-million times less sensitive than performing a proton ($^1$H) experiment~\cite{degraafMagneticFieldDependence2020,laddProsConsUltrahighfield2018}. For this reason, \gls{DMI} is typically performed at \gls{UHF} to leverage the supra-linear SNR boost (SNR $\propto B_0^{1.7}$)\cite{pohmannSignaltonoiseRatioMR2016,degraafMagneticFieldDependence2020}.

 To offset the reduced sensitivity, \gls{DMI} protocols typically use voxel volumes $\approx 1000$-fold larger, acquisition times $\approx 100$-fold longer, and acquisition weighting strategies to improve \gls{SNR}. Acquisition-weighted spoiled \gls{GE}-\gls{CSI} together with established spectral fitting methods\cite{reederIterativeDecompositionWater2005,vanhammeImprovedMethodAccurate1997,purvisOXSAOpensourceMagnetic2017,purvisOXSAOpensourceMagnetic2017} is the preferred method for performing \gls{DMI} investigations due to its availability and ease of data interpretation. The nominal spatio-temporal resolution of a typical human brain \gls{DMI} measurement ranges from 3 mL in 10 min at 9.4 T to 14 mL in 39 min at 3 T~\cite{ruhmDeuteriumMetabolicImaging2021, defeyterDeuteriumMetabolicImaging2018, seresroigDeuteriumMetabolicImaging2023, niessWholebrainDeuteriumMetabolic2024, adamsonDeuteriumMetabolicImaging2024}. Acquisition weighting, in addition to optimizing the \gls{PSF} shape, introduces additional blurring, increasing the reported nominal resolution by $\approx 6$–$7$-fold. With \gls{PET} resolution in the range of $100 \mu L$, recent research focuses on improving \gls{DMI} sensitivity to achieve higher resolutions. Due to the short $T_1$ relaxation times resulting from quadrupolar relaxation and relatively high $T_2$ relaxation times of deuterium metabolites, recent pre-clinical studies \cite{petersImprovingDeuteriumMetabolic2021,montraziHighsensitivityDeuteriumMetabolic2023} investigated the use of \gls{bSSFP} acquisition to improve signal efficiency.  Researchers have also investigated denoising approaches utilizing prior information about spatial smoothness, kinetic models of the metabolites, or machine learning models combining both\cite{montraziDeuteriumImagingWarburg2023,osburgPhysicsInformedDeepAutoencoder2025}.
